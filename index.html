🔑 Why passkeys can't be phished

  1. A passkey is locked to a domain. When you register, the secret key is bound to the exact site (e.g. coinbase.com) — and stored in your device's secure hardware.
  2. Your browser checks the domain for you. It will only offer a passkey whose domain matches the site you're on. A homoglyph look-alike like cоinbase.com (Cyrillic “о”) is a different domain at the byte level — so it gets nothing.
  3. So the fake site gets nothing. The phishing page can copy every pixel, but it can't fake the domain your passkey is bound to. The browser simply finds no key — and that's your signal.
  4. Nothing to steal, nothing to type. There's no password to phish, no code to read out. The private key never leaves your device.

This demo reproduces that exact behavior: in passkey mode, the fake site reports "no passkey for this domain" — just like a real browser would.
